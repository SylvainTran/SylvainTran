Design Philosophy

Process

When designing, I focus on the core experience that I want the user to think about. I gather references of similar levels or environments to get inspired, and I put them in a pure ref board to consult them easily. I use rapid prototyping to iterate, and I get and use data to answer design questions.

This process is repeated until the desired result is obtained.

I usually start with fast ideation of high level concepts that I would like to test on a sheet of paper, then I try to do a paper prototype on a grid sheet.

When the initial ideas are clear and I start to understand how I can relate the concepts to my technical knowledge at a more intuitive level, I fire up a game engine and start translating my paper prototypes in the editor. I will start by greyboxing fast and doing some adjustments (lookdev). After I get an overall picture that I'm satisfied with, I start adding primitive placeholders for the actors in the level, and I look for re-usable code that I can use to get started quickly.

As I work in the game engine, I start to make a list of requirements that emerge from my bottom-up prototyping. Usually at the end of this phase, I run into a major design question or problem, for which I require user testing to answer. I get my level out, and ask someone to try it. I design a questionaire using known research methods and questionaire design, making sure my questions do not induce any biases or become overloaded. I use ratings that can be measured (i.e., Likert scales), and I try to get a decent sample size of players. After a few rounds of testing, I revise and make adjustments, plus add a few of my own touches.

Some rules and heuristics I tend to follow to illustrate my process:

Problem: A mechanic, encounter or part of the level does not feel interesting.
Rule: Before getting into scripting, can I use VFX and animations to get some oomph or juice factor? Can I find solutions in art, or how I present the information to the player?

Problem: The experience seems to lack for something.
Rule: Can I vary variables that I already have at hand? Can I think of small variations that could make the experience more interesting or challenging before adding something else?

Problem Solving Using Analytical and Scientific Methods

Given my background in Computer Science and Engineering, as well as Design, Computation Arts and Psychology, I am well versed in several different iterative workflows. I draw from research and design methods, and I may use statistical analysis to balance game mechanics or levels if possible.

I also incorporate my research background in the prototyping process, when designing UX questionaires and when validating test results.
